### 1.2 The Operations Strategy (OS) Framework (Recap)

The OS Framework connects Business Strategy to Operations Strategy through two main pillars:

**Resources (Asset Portfolio) -- Key Questions:**

| Decision Area | Key Question |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Size** | How many resources should we invest? |
| **Timing** | When should we increase or reduce resources? |
| **Type** | What type of resources are best? |
| **Location** | Where to locate resources? |

**Processes (Activity Network) -- Key Questions:**

| Decision Area | Key Question |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Supply** | What activities should we perform internally? |
| **Technology** | Which process technology do our processes need? |
| **Innovation** | When and how to innovate and improve? |
| **Demand** | How to match demand with available supply? |

**The Flow:**
- **Business Strategy** drives the **Operations Strategy**
- Operations Strategy determines **Resources** and **Processes**
- Resources and Processes produce **Capabilities**: Cost, Quality, Lead Time (LT), Flexibility
- Capabilities support the **Value Proposition**: Price, Quality, Responsiveness, Variety

### 1.3 Concept of Operational Trade-offs

**Definition:** A trade-off is "a balancing of factors all of which are not attainable at the same time; a giving up of one thing in return for another."

**Operational Trade-offs Defined:** Operational capabilities (Cost, Quality, Lead Time, Flexibility) are governed by trade-offs that are defined by the resources and the processes -- i.e., the operational systems.

**Three Critical Implications:**

1. **An organization cannot have it all** -- its capabilities exhibit trade-offs due to the finite abilities of its operational system
2. **Operations strategy can shape and change the trade-offs** by choosing the right operational systems
3. **Operational trade-offs require an organization to make choices** -- you must decide where on the trade-off curve to operate

---

### 1.4 Trade-off Curves

**Definition:** A trade-off curve is the graphical representation of the trade-offs inherent in an operational system.

**Key Properties:**
- The choice of operational system shapes a company's capability trade-offs (Cost vs. Quality vs. LT vs. Flexibility)
- Trade-off curves can be presented as **iso-NPV curves** -- curves showing capability combinations that yield a given NPV (also called indifference curves for the same NPV)
- For simplicity, the class uses **2-dimensional trade-off curves** (typically Unit Cost vs. a Non-cost competency like Flexibility/Product Variety)

**Example -- Three Process Types and Their Trade-off Curves:**

| Process Type | Unit Cost | Flexibility/Product Variety | Changeover Cost |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| **Job Shop** | Highest unit cost | Highest flexibility | Lowest changeover cost |
| **Batch Process** | In between | In between | In between |
| **Repetitive Line** | Lowest unit cost | Lowest flexibility | Highest changeover cost |

**Graphical Representation:**
- X-axis: Product Variety / Flexibility (N) -- ranging from 1 to 100
- Y-axis: Unit Cost (C) -- inverted scale, so lower cost is "better" (higher on graph)
- Each process type traces its own curve; moving along a curve represents trade-offs within that system
- Moving between curves represents choosing a fundamentally different operational system

---

### 1.5 The Efficient Frontier

**Definition:** The efficient frontier is a snapshot that traces the industry's lowest costs to produce specific non-cost-based capability bundles (e.g., quality, lead time, and flexibility).

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Sweet Spot:** Each operational system has a "sweet spot" -- an area on its trade-off curve where it operates on the efficient frontier. Outside this sweet spot, the system is less efficient relative to alternatives.

2. **Not-Attainable Region:** Above the efficient frontier lies a set of competency combinations that are not currently attainable by any existing operational system in the industry.

3. **Constructing the Frontier:** The efficient frontier is formed by the envelope (outer boundary) of all individual operational systems' trade-off curves in the industry.

**Visual Description:**
- The efficient frontier is the outermost boundary curve formed by connecting the sweet spots of Job Shop (high variety, high cost), Batch Process (medium variety, medium cost), and Repetitive Line (low variety, low cost)
- Points inside the frontier are attainable but inefficient
- Points outside the frontier are not currently attainable

---

### 1.6 Operational Efficiency (OE)

**Definition:** A non-cost-based capability's operational efficiency (OE) is measured by its **cost disadvantage relative to the efficient frontier** for that same capability.

**Measuring OE -- The COE Concept:**
- **COE** = the largest cost advantage any competitor would have over us, were they to use their operational systems to deliver the same (non-cost) capabilities as we do
- COE measures how much more efficiently a rival's operational system can deliver the same value proposition

**Two Possible Outcomes:**

1. **If COE is zero or negative:** We are operationally efficient -- no competitor can deliver our capabilities at lower cost
2. **If COE is positive:** We are operationally inefficient at the rate of the cost disadvantage of our most threatening competitor

**Why COE Matters:**
- COE is the operational efficiency-driven cost differential
- It measures how one company is more efficient in their operational systems than another, even if both were to run at their desired utilizations and provide identical value propositions
- The seriousness of a competitive threat is directly related to COE

---

### 1.7 OE Improvement

**How OE Improvement Works:**
- OE improvement **moves the trade-off curve outwards** (away from the origin)
- This creates **new strategic options**: a firm can choose to either increase differentiation OR improve cost-efficiency (or a combination)

**Example -- Setup Time Reduction:**
- Reducing setup times shifts the trade-off curve outward
- The firm can then choose to use the improvement for:
  - **More variety** at the same cost (move rightward along the new curve)
  - **Lower cost** at the same variety (move upward along the new curve)
  - **A combination** of both
- Both paths can lead to a reduction of COE (if COE was positive to start with)

---

### 1.8 Operational Focus

**Definition:** A focused operation is one whose required capabilities are restricted to a narrow set in the capability space of costs, quality, lead time, and flexibility.

**Why Focus Works -- "Simplicity and Repetition Breeds Competence":**
- **Learning and optimization** -- doing fewer things allows deeper expertise
- **Less divergence in attention spans** -- resources are not pulled in conflicting directions
- No compromises are necessary in the design of the operational system

**Focus on the Trade-off Curve:**
- A **focused operation** has a consistent operating point on its trade-off curve (tight cluster of operating points)
- An **unfocused operation** has operating points scattered over a large region on its trade-off curve (wide dispersion)

**How to Focus -- The "Divide and Conquer" Approach:**

**Step 1:** Partition products and services into groups with homogeneous capability needs
- Example: Group products into clusters that need similar combinations of cost, quality, flexibility

**Step 2:** For each group, separate those resources and processes that critically support its key value proposition while sharing the others
- Dedicated resources for what matters most to each group
- Shared resources where sharing does not compromise focus

**Step 3:** Create a charter for each operation to retain focus over time
- Prevents mission creep and scope expansion

**Degree of Focus:**
- There is a spectrum from "one single system shared by all groups" (unfocused) to "completely separate systems, each focused on one group" (fully focused)
- The optimal solution is often a **tailored degree of focus** -- not fully shared, not fully separated

---

### 1.9 Challenges Related to Operational Focus

1. **Pressure to grow** leads to inclusion of products/services with different customer needs
   - Example: Dell selling laptop computers (expanding beyond its focused desktop business)

2. **Changes in customer needs or competitor positions** may lead to using operational systems in ways they were not designed for
   - Example: Marks & Spencer trying to deliver fast fashion with systems designed for traditional fashion

3. **Focus does not require a narrow product mix** -- it is possible to focus on variety; many firms successfully do so
   - Example: Three-star Michelin restaurants constantly change menus but are very focused in what they try to achieve

4. **Operational efficiency is necessary but not sufficient** for NPV maximization. The operation must also:
   - Be **sufficiently different** from competitors to avoid direct competition
   - Be **difficult to copy** by competitors
   - Produce products/services that are **preferred by a large enough customer segment**
   - Example of failure: Swiss watchmakers in the 1970s were efficient but lost to quartz technology

5. **Economies of scale consideration:** If large economies of scale can be leveraged, one combined unfocused system may still generate more value than two separated focused systems
   - Essential question: To what extent can assets and processes be shared? What are the benefits and costs of sharing (economies of scale and scope)?

6. **Operational focus can lead to rigidity**
   - Example: Ford had a hard time adjusting to changes in customer preferences after its highly focused Model T success (starting in 1908)

---

### 1.10 Analyzing Competitive Threats Using Trade-off Curves

**The Core Framework for Threat Analysis:**

When comparing two competitors (e.g., "us" vs. "rival"), the total observed cost difference (Delta C) can be decomposed into three components:

#### The Cost Decomposition Formula:

```
Delta C + Cv,rival = Cv,us + COE + CS
```

Rearranging to solve for COE:

```
COE = Delta C + Cv,rival - Cv,us - CS
```

Where:
- **Delta C** = Total observed cost difference (rival's cost minus our cost, or vice versa)
- **Cv** = Volume (utilization) cost differential
- **CS** = Competitive strategy-driven cost differential
- **COE** = Operational efficiency cost differential (the key metric)

#### Detailed Definition of Each Component:

**Delta C (Total Cost Difference):**
- The raw, unadjusted cost difference between two firms
- Calculated by comparing costs at current operating conditions
- NOT the meaningful number for threat assessment -- must be decomposed

**Cv -- Volume (Utilization) Cost Differential:**
- The extra costs incurred by operating at less than the strategically-targeted utilization level
- Caused by: economic downturns, market misses, demand declines
- Arises because fixed costs are spread over fewer units than planned
- Must be calculated for BOTH firms (Cv,us and Cv,rival)
- **Calculation approach:** Compare actual per-unit costs at current utilization to what per-unit costs would be at the strategically planned utilization, holding total cost constant

**CS -- Competitive Strategy-Driven Cost Differential:**
- The cost difference inherent in the way each company chooses to compete
- A strategy emphasizing product innovation and customer responsiveness is expected to be more costly than a pure low-cost strategy
- Customized products cost more than standardized ones
- This is a "legitimate" cost difference -- it reflects a deliberate strategic choice, not inefficiency

**COE -- Operational Efficiency Cost Differential:**
- The remaining cost difference after controlling for location, volume, and strategy
- This is the TRUE measure of how much more efficiently one company's operational systems work compared to another's
- A positive COE means we are operationally inefficient relative to the rival
- This is the number that determines the seriousness of the competitive threat

#### Why This Decomposition Matters:

- **To estimate COE, we must control for location, volume, and strategy**
- Without these adjustments, raw cost comparisons are misleading
- A firm may appear to have higher costs, but much of the difference could be due to legitimate strategic choices (CS) or temporary volume shortfalls (Cv) rather than true operational inefficiency (COE)

---

### 1.11 Guidelines for Operations-Based Competition

1. **Study your own and your rivals' trade-off curves**
   - Conduct threat analysis by estimating COE
   - Be careful: adjust for location, volume/utilization, and strategy before concluding about COE

2. **Strengthen your competitive position**
   - Focus operations appropriately
   - Manage supplier and buyer relations
   - Manage labor and capital markets
   - These actions increase CS (the legitimate cost of your differentiated strategy) and thereby reduce COE

3. **Anticipate customer dynamics**
   - A longer-term strategy should include market research to estimate customer utility dynamics
   - This helps manage Cv (volume risk) by ensuring demand matches capacity

4. **Design strategies to improve competitive advantage through operations**
   - Use focus judiciously
   - Adopt best practices from competitors
   - Develop proprietary practices in support of a differentiated and attractive value proposition

---

### 1.12 Summary of Key Concepts

**Operational Trade-off:**
- Simple adjustments in the use of operational systems will reveal trade-offs between performance on different competency dimensions
- Across existing operational systems in an industry, trade-offs between competencies exist (forming the efficient frontier)
- Operational systems provide competitive advantage when their use is operationally efficient, generates products that a large enough customer segment is willing to pay for, and when they are sufficiently different from and hard to copy by rivals

**Analyzing Competitive Threats:**
- Do not use cost numbers at face value
- Adjust for location, strategic positioning, and volume (utilization) differences to arrive at the cost advantage due to operational efficiency (COE), which is the meaningful "net cost advantage" number

**Operational Focus:**
- Is achieved when an operational system is used to deliver a narrow set of competency bundles (does NOT require a narrow product mix)
- Leads to operational efficiency because practice perfects and no compromises are necessary in the design of the operational system
- Is hard to maintain (pressure to grow, customer needs change over time)
- Is no guarantee for success (focus is necessary but not sufficient)
- Can be applied judiciously to maximize NPV
- One combined unfocused system may generate more value than two separated focused systems (when economies of scale dominate)

### 2.3 DJC's Kawasaki Plant -- Detailed Operations Profile

**Vision and Goals (Mr. Esaka's "Ultimate Rationalization of Mass Production"):**
1. Asset utilization of 100%
2. Raw material yield of 99%
3. Customer complaints not to exceed 1 per million units of output
- Cost was not an explicit goal but understood as a natural outcome of meeting the above three goals

**Product Technology:**
- Product designs thoroughly analyzed for manufacturability before production begins
- Standardized product design to reduce the number of product variations (only 640 SKUs)
- Used tin plating instead of gold where possible (tin is cheaper, works well in low-power applications)
- Packaged connectors ONLY on tape and reels (standardized packaging)
- Extensive value engineering to identify and implement cost-saving design changes without compromising quality
- If Kawasaki used ACC's product design and packaging, material costs would be $20.90/thousand units; through value engineering, actual Kawasaki costs were reduced to $14.89/thousand

**Process Technology -- Key Principles:**

1. **Pre-automation:** Significant resources devoted to making the production process suitable for high automation BEFORE automating. Steps included:
   - Designing out sources of process variation
   - Simplifying material handling
   - Mistake-proofing operations

2. **Integrated production lines:** Unlike ACC's functional layout, DJC organized into 4 product-family cells, each with 2-6 parallel lines. Each line has its own stamping, molding, assembly, and packaging (only plating is shared across cells)

3. **Molding excellence:**
   - Molding group included experts in polymer physics and former mold manufacturer employees
   - Rigorous mold maintenance: full maintenance every 6 months + daily basic maintenance
   - Molds replaced every 3 years average (vs. 8 years at ACC)
   - Average annual cost per mold: $29,000 (vs. $40,000 at ACC)
   - Mold yields: greater than 99.99%

4. **In-house technology development:**
   - Built equipment internally because they could not afford to buy it early on
   - Learned from Toyota and other Japanese role models
   - Strategic motivation: in Japan, contractual agreements preventing equipment resale to competitors were uncommon, so buying equipment meant competitors could get the same technology
   - Proprietary equipment provided a competitive moat

**Quality Control:**
- Five objectives: improve QC standards, improve process inspection, improve precision of molded components, improve quality of product designs, reduce waste
- SPC (Statistical Process Control) integrated throughout
- Defect rate: 1 per million units
- 99% raw material yield achieved
- Quality through PREVENTION, not inspection

**Sourcing:**
- Close relationships with few key suppliers
- Suppliers had to meet rigorous quality standards and certify quality of every delivery
- Incoming materials used directly in production WITHOUT further inspection
- Suppliers expected to continuously improve quality over time
- JIT delivery: daily or weekly basis; largest customers received shipments every few hours

**Production and Inventory Control:**
- Long production runs to minimize yield and capacity losses from changeovers
- Low WIP (work-in-process) inventory
- Standardized packaging (tape and reels only)

**Workforce:**
- 94 employees total (vs. 396 at ACC Sunnyvale)
- Goal: gradually reduce direct production workers through increased automation
- Hiring: new graduates from high schools and universities
- Wages above average for new graduates but below average for experienced workers (encouraging turnover around age 35)
- Average employee tenure: 9 years (vs. industry average of longer)
- Extensive training through formal programs and job rotation (every 3 years)
- Production workers responsible for ALL activities affecting conversion and material flows (not specialized)
- Multi-functional workers, not specialized to particular processes

**Labor Composition (Kawasaki, 94 employees):**

| Category | Kawasaki % | Sunnyvale % |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Control (Indirect) | 11.7% | 16.7% |
| Technology Development (Indirect) | 12.8% | 6.8% |
| Materials Handling (Indirect) | 3.2% | 10.4% |
| Mechanics (Indirect) | 4.3% | 11.9% |
| Direct Labor (Production) | 68.0% | 54.0% |

Key insight: DJC has a much higher proportion of direct labor (68% vs 54%) and much lower indirect labor, especially in materials handling (3.2% vs 10.4%) and mechanics (4.3% vs 11.9%). DJC invests more in technology development (12.8% vs 6.8%).

### 2.4 ACC's Sunnyvale Plant -- Detailed Operations Profile

**History:**
- Opened in 1961 to serve Silicon Valley electronics industries
- Started in a small leased facility (1 million connectors/year capacity)
- Moved to a newly built facility in 1963 as demand accelerated
- By 1991, one of the largest connector plants in North America

**Capacity and Utilization:**
- Designed capacity: 600 million units
- Planned utilization: 85%
- Actual utilization: 70% (producing ~420 million units)
- No plans to change capacity

**Product Strategy:**
- 4,500 different models (vs. DJC's 640 SKUs)
- Custom orders: 15% of total production volume
- Quality defined as conforming to customers' needs, not just manufacturing specs
- Engineers collaborated with customers early in design cycle
- Many custom products became industry standards
- Emphasis on product technology and innovation

**Process Layout -- Functional/Disconnected Layout:**
- 5 production areas organized by FUNCTION (not product family):
  1. Terminal stamping and fabrication
  2. Terminal plating
  3. Plastic housing molding
  4. Assembly and testing
  5. Packaging
- Each functional area produces ALL product types
- WIP buffers between areas support batch production

**Production Flow:**
1. Batch of terminals cut and stamped from metal wire/strips
2. Terminals transported to holding area awaiting plating
3. Meanwhile, molding department fabricates plastic housings
4. Housings sent to WIP holding area (housings ready before plating completes)
5. Plated terminals and housings sent to assembly
6. Automated assembly for most products; manual assembly for very low volume products (~10% of total)
7. Final testing and packaging (multiple packaging formats)

**Quality:**
- Quality was traditionally defined as: deliver nearly perfect connectors and never miss a scheduled delivery
- Defect rate: ~26,000 per million units (vs. DJC's 1 per million)
- Quality approach: inspection-based ("We do it the old fashioned way -- we inspect it")
- Some attempts to implement SPC but met with resistance
- Yields on new products as low as 55%; improved to ~98% after one year in production

**WIP and Inventory:**
- High WIP historically not viewed as a problem (covered by growing sales)
- Average molding production run: 1.5 days
- WIP provided flexibility for unexpected orders without holding finished goods inventory
- Finance pushing for shorter runs, but shorter runs hurt utilization figures
- Growing tension between WIP reduction goals and production efficiency

**Workforce Issues:**
- 396 employees (vs. 94 at DJC)
- Higher proportion of indirect labor (46% vs. 32%)
- Particularly high in materials handling (10.4% vs 3.2%) and mechanics (11.9% vs 4.3%)
- Low investment in technology development (6.8% vs 12.8%)

**Recent Challenges:**
- Costs increasing while quality deteriorating
- Gross margins eroding (52% to 43% over 7 years)
- Invested several hundred million dollars in plants/equipment (1983-1988) but competition and slowing demand prevented reaching growth/profit goals
- Process technology not updated since 1988

### 2.6 Analysis Approach -- Decomposing the Cost Differential

**Step-by-step approach from the slides (using labor cost as the detailed example):**

**Raw Data:**
- ACC labor cost: $10.30/thousand units at 70% utilization (420M units produced; capacity = 600M)
- DJC labor cost: $4.15/thousand units at 87.5% utilization (700M units produced; capacity = 800M)
- Observed labor cost difference: CLabor = $10.30 - $4.15 = **$6.15**

**Step 1: Calculate Volume Effects (Cv)**

For ACC:
- Total labor bill = $10.30/thousand x 420M units = $4.3M
- At planned 85% utilization: would produce 510M units
- Labor cost at planned utilization = $4.3M / 510M = $8.43/thousand
- **Cv,ACC = $10.30 - $8.43 = $1.87** (cost penalty from operating below planned utilization)

For DJC:
- Total labor bill = $4.15/thousand x 700M units = $2.905M
- At planned 100% utilization: would produce 800M units
- Labor cost at planned utilization = $2.905M / 800M = $3.63/thousand
- **Cv,DJC = $4.15 - $3.63 = $0.52** (cost penalty from operating below planned utilization)

**Step 2: Calculate Strategy Effects (CS)**

If DJC enters the US market and operates at 85% utilization like ACC (same strategic operating conditions):
- Labor cost = $2.905M / (0.85 x 800M) = $4.27/thousand
- **CS,DJC = $4.27 - $3.63 = $0.64**

**Step 3: Calculate COE**

Using the formula: COE = Delta C + Cv,rival - Cv,us - CS

```
COE = CLabor + Cv,DJC - Cv,ACC - CS,DJC
COE = $6.15 + $0.52 - $1.87 - $0.64
COE = $4.16
```

**Interpretation:** After controlling for volume effects and strategic positioning, DJC still has a **$4.16/thousand unit** cost advantage in labor alone due to pure operational efficiency. This represents a significant and genuine competitive threat.

**Sources of the Major DJC Cost Savings (from the $13.54 overall location-adjusted difference):**
- **Raw Materials:** Product and packaging design (value engineering)
- **Labor:** Automation and multi-functional training
- **Depreciation:** High utilization of capacity (asset fully depreciated by 1991)
- **Other:** Possibly lower overhead (case does not specify)

### 2.8 Assessing Alignment -- Product-Process Matrix

**DJC's Layout: Connected Line Flow by Product Family**
- Plant divided into 4 cells (A, B, C, D)
- Each cell has 2 to 6 parallel lines
- Each line has its own stamping, molding, assembly, and packaging
- Only plating is shared across cells
- This is a **repetitive line** process -- highly aligned with standardized, high-volume production

**ACC's Layout: Disconnected Line Flow (Functional)**
- Plant divided into 5 large functional areas
- Each functional area produces ALL product types
- WIP buffers between areas support batch production
- This is a **batch process** layout

**Product-Process Matrix Positioning:**

| | Customized Solutions <---> Standardized Products |
|---|---|
| **Job Shop (High Flexibility)** | ACC's custom segment (15% of volume) |
| **Batch** | ACC's main operation (positioned here) |
| **Repetitive Line (Low Flexibility)** | DJC (positioned here) |

**Key Alignment Insight:**
- DJC is well-aligned: standardized products on a repetitive line (diagonal of the matrix)
- ACC is **misaligned** for the majority of its business: 85% of its products are standard, yet it uses a batch process designed for variety. Only the 15% custom segment truly requires batch flexibility
- ACC is trying to serve two different market segments (custom and standard) with one operational system -- this is the definition of being unfocused

### 2.9 Takeaways and Lessons Learned

#### Case Learning Points:

**1. Assessing Competitive Operations Strategies:**
- ACC and DJC have chosen fundamentally different competitive strategies, resources, and processes
- DJC offers a standard product portfolio and sells a "manufacturing capability" -- it is also a formidable learner (continuous improvement)
- ACC offers design and manufacturing customization and sells "customer solutions" -- but is NOT well-aligned for the large majority (85%) of its business
- Analytical tools: OS framework elements + alignment analysis (product-process matrix)

**2. Valuing an Operations Differentiation Strategy -- Must Correct for:**
1. **Location** differences (raw material costs, labor costs, etc.)
2. **Volume** and other deviations from planned operating conditions (utilization gaps)
3. **Strategic positioning** (cost of delivering different value propositions)
4. The remaining cost differential is attributable to **operational efficiency** (COE)

This decomposition quantifies the trade-offs between competing on cost vs. competing on innovation/flexibility.

**3. Designing Strategic Defense:**

On the **operations side:**
- Definitely implement OE improvement activities to eliminate COE and safeguard ACC's custom segment
- The magnitude of different cost items dictates the priority of implementation
- Consider the "standard segment": Is the current process the best fit? Should ACC implement a separate tailored process for standards? Or exit the standard segment entirely?

On the **value side:**
- How can ACC improve the value it delivers to customers?

On the **competitive side:**
- Thwart DJC entry: prevent them from gathering scale in the U.S. market
- Lock in customers through relationships and customization

**4. Critical Insights on Benchmarking:**
- Benchmarking must correct for inherent differences between "apples and oranges"
- Sometimes companies that look like competitors may not actually be direct competitors (they serve different segments with different strategies)
- It is critical to sort out differences due to **strategy** from those due to **pure inefficiency**
- Without controlling for strategy differences, the competitive situation may look worse or better than it actually is
- Use benchmarking to target those capabilities that need improvement to better carry out your strategy

## PART 3: EXAM PREPARATION -- KEY FORMULAS AND FRAMEWORKS TO REMEMBER

---

### 3.1 The Cost Decomposition Formula

```
COE = Delta_C + Cv,rival - Cv,us - CS
```

Where:
- Delta_C = Total observed cost difference (our cost minus rival's cost)
- Cv,us = Our volume/utilization cost penalty (actual cost minus cost at planned utilization)
- Cv,rival = Rival's volume/utilization cost penalty
- CS = Strategy-driven cost differential (cost of our differentiation vs. rival's approach)
- COE = Operational efficiency cost differential (the TRUE threat indicator)

### 3.2 Calculating Volume Cost Differential (Cv)

```
Cv = Actual cost per unit - Cost per unit at planned utilization
```

Where cost per unit at planned utilization = Total cost / (Planned utilization rate x Capacity)

### 3.3 The Product-Process Matrix

```
                    Low Volume,        Medium Volume,      High Volume,
                    One-of-a-kind      Several Products    Few Major Products    Commodity
Job Shop            [ALIGNED]
Batch                                  [ALIGNED]
Repetitive Line                                            [ALIGNED]
Continuous Flow                                                                  [ALIGNED]
```

Firms positioned OFF the diagonal are misaligned -- either investing too much in flexibility (above diagonal) or too little (below diagonal).

### 3.4 The Three Guidelines for Operations-Based Competition

1. **Study trade-off curves** of yourself and rivals (estimate COE)
2. **Strengthen competitive position** through focus, supplier relations, and proprietary practices (increase CS, reduce COE)
3. **Anticipate customer dynamics** through market research (manage Cv)

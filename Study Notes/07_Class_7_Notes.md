### 3. Product Technology

**Definition:** Product technology refers to the scientific and technical knowledge embedded in the products themselves.

**Examples of product technology grounded in quantum mechanics:**
- **Transistor** -- e.g., the Bulova electrochromic watch was one of the first consumer products to use a transistor.
- **Electron microscope** -- often used for quality control and failure analysis in manufacturing.
- **Flash memory chips** found in USB drives -- uses quantum tunnelling to erase their memory cells.

**Connection to course cases:**
- **Consumer electronics:** Swiss Watch industry, ACC/DJC connectors.
- **Biotech and Pharmaceutical:** Eli Lilly, Moderna.

---

### 4. Process Technology

**Definition:** Process technology refers to the technology used in manufacturing, transportation, and coordination of operations.

**Three categories of process technology:**
1. **Manufacturing technology** -- the equipment and methods used to make products.
2. **Transportation technology** -- how goods and materials move through supply chains.
3. **Coordination technology** -- how activities are synchronized and managed.

**GPS as an example of coordination technology:**
- Exploits quantum mechanics and Special/General Relativity via microwave atomic clocks.
- Works through triangulation with signals from synchronized atomic clocks on orbiting satellites.
- Precision is approximately 1 meter.
- NASA's Deep Space Atomic Clock: off by less than a nanosecond after four days and less than a microsecond after 10 years.
- Optical atomic clocks (2017 application): clock at 4 x 10^14 Hz with uncertainty less than 1 second in the age of the universe.

**Connection to cases:** Watch, connectors, drugs and vaccines.

**Related manufacturing technologies (from DAO2703):** CAD, CAM, CAPP, NC, CNC, AGV, AS/RS, CIM, CNC machines, IoT, FMS, Drones, Robots, etc.

### 5. Process Technology Decisions

#### 5a. Link Between Product Technology and Process Development

A critical operations strategy decision is how to link product development with process development.

**Diagram concept:** Product Development and Process Development are shown as two parallel streams that must be linked.

**Methods for integrating product and process development:**
- **Simultaneous (or concurrent) engineering** -- developing product and process in parallel rather than sequentially.
- **Design for Manufacturing (DFM)** -- product design rules that make manufacturing easier and more efficient.
- **Design for Six Sigma** -- designing products to minimize variation and defects from the outset.
- **Manufacturing's involvement in prototyping** -- bringing manufacturing expertise into early product design stages.

**What is at stake when linking product and process technology:**
- **Time** -- faster or slower time to market.
- **Costs and yield** -- manufacturing efficiency and waste reduction.
- **Conformance quality** -- how well the final product matches design specifications.
- **Volume** -- ability to scale (e.g., Pandemic vs. Endemic demand scenarios).

#### 5b. Match Process Technology with Product Life Cycle Stage

**Key concept:** As a product matures through its technology life cycle, the process technology should evolve accordingly.

**Diagram description (Product Life Cycle & Process Technology):**
- **Early stage (high demand variability):** Process should have **flexibility** to allow for product innovation. Product designs are still changing, so flexible processes are needed.
- **Later stage (product design stabilizes):** Invest in **automation** to reduce costs. Automated, connected, and dedicated processes become appropriate.

**Properties of automated, connected, and dedicated processes:**
- Less flexible, but this is less problematic because product designs have stabilized.
- Can deliver **lower variable costs** and thus **lower average costs** as production volumes increase.

**The tradeoff:** Early on, you need flexibility for innovation; later, you want efficiency through automation.

#### 5c. Centralized vs. Decentralized Process Development

**Decision framework for process development location:**

- **Centralized Process Development:** All process development happens in one location, then transferred to plants.
- **Decentralized Process Development:** Each plant (e.g., offshore plant, domestic plant) develops its own processes.

**Key questions in the centralization decision:**
- **Resources?** -- Where are the engineering resources located?
- **Time?** -- How quickly can processes be developed and transferred?
- **Authority?** -- Who has decision-making power over process choices?

**Advantages of centralization (and enabled standardization):**
- Can be important for **global customers** who need consistent products.
- Can lead to **efficiencies**: shared learning, less duplication of effort, ability to move production load between plants.

**Disadvantages of centralization:**
- Can make it more **difficult to adapt to local input factors** (labor skills, raw materials) and **output requirements** (local market preferences, regulations).

#### 5d. Learning Before Doing vs. Learning By Doing (in Process Technology Context)

**Diagram: Lab --> Transfer Time --> Full-Scale Operations**

- **Lab (Learning Before Doing):** Experiments, simulations, pilot studies done before full-scale production.
- **Full-Scale Operations (Learning By Doing):** Learning happens on the production floor through actual experience.
- **Transfer Time:** The gap between lab development and full-scale production.

**Timing of transfer depends on three factors:**
1. **Depth of process knowledge** -- how well you understand the underlying process science.
2. **Fidelity of experiments** -- the ability of the "lab" to simulate manufacturing-scale behavior accurately.
3. **Cost of experiments** -- relative cost of lab experiments versus full-scale trials.

### 6. Innovation vs. Improvement

**Innovation** = creating something new.
- Can be both significant and incremental.
- May or may not accumulate to radical change.

**Improvement** = making something better.
- Can be both radical (reengineering) and continuous (Lean and Six Sigma).

**Both are processes that result in change.** The difference lies in:
- The **implied extent of the change**.
- The **time scale** on which the change occurs.

**Key principle:** Building capabilities for improvement and innovation must be an integral part of the theory and practice of operations strategy. The fundamental question in operations strategy is how to stay ahead and adapt and align competencies to changing customer preferences.

### 8. Continuous Improvement (CI)

**When to use:** Seeking steady, incremental change. Projects are typically **low risk**.

**Four guiding principles of Continuous Improvement:**

1. **Scientific method:** Any improvement must be made in accordance with the scientific method -- generate hypotheses, test, then implement.
2. **Reduce process variability:** All work is highly specified as to **content, sequence, timing, and outcome** to eliminate problems.
3. **Employee empowerment:** All employees are empowered to make improvements; managers serve as **coaches** (not commanders).
4. **Built-in tests (controls):** All activities, connections, and flow paths have built-in tests to signal problems automatically.

*(Adapted from: Spears, "The DNA of the Toyota Production System," Harvard Business Review, 1999)*

---

### 9. Continuous Improvement as a Process

**The Ideal Operation:**
- Perfectly synchronized with demand.
- At lowest cost.

**D = deviation from ideal = waste, variability, inflexibility = opportunity for improvement.**

**The CI Process has two main activities:**

**Activity 1: Increase Visibility of D (Deviations)**
- **Process measurement** -- quantifying performance gaps.
- **Visual management** -- making problems visible to all workers.
- **Exploratory stress** -- deliberately stressing the system (e.g., reducing inventory buffers) to reveal hidden problems.

**Activity 2: Reduce D (Deviations)**
- **Root cause analysis & problem-solving mindset** -- don't just fix symptoms; find and fix the underlying cause.
- **Waste reduction (Lean tools)** -- eliminate non-value-adding activities.
- **Variability reduction (Six Sigma, TQM)** -- reduce variation in processes and outputs.

---

### 10. Copy Best Practice

**When to use:** Trying to replicate an active best practice representing a large, breakthrough improvement (e.g., delayed differentiation, internet store). These are **large projects with medium risk**.

**Guidelines for copying best practice:**
1. Make sure the system **can be copied** and **is worth copying**.
2. Work from a **single, active template** (the process you want to copy).
3. Copy **as closely and completely as you can** -- including plant & equipment, skill sets, and procedures.
4. **Don't reformulate, adapt, or improve** upon the practice until you have reached acceptable results.
5. When problems occur, **refer back to the active template**.

**Important warnings:**
- Neither people nor documents can fully explain how a complex system works.
- We are often **overconfident** about our understanding of why something works.

*(Adapted from: Szulanski & Winter, 2002, "Getting It Right the Second Time," Harvard Business Review)*

---

### 11. Reengineering

**When to use:** Trying to achieve **breakthrough, superior performance** with a new, unproven process. These are potentially **large projects with high risk**.

**Guidelines for reengineering projects:**
1. **Identify knowledge gaps** and learn proactively.
2. Use a **stage-gate process** to start small and reduce risk quickly before committing large resources.

**Stage-gate process (diagram):**
- **Feasibility Studies** --> **Product Prototype** --> **Pilot Plant** --> **Full-Scale Plant**

3. Use **simulation** to test new products/processes and avoid errors.
4. Use **partners/contractors** to manage risk.

**Sources cited:**
- Loch, De Meyer, and Pich (2006), *Managing the Unknown: A New Approach to Managing High Uncertainty and Risk in Projects*.
- Hammer and Champy (1993), *Reengineering the Corporation*.

### 12. Learning and Improvement

**Organizational learning** is directly related to both improvement and innovation.

The literature breaks organizational learning into two activities:
1. **Conceptual learning** -- understanding the "why" behind processes.
2. **Operational learning** -- understanding the "how" of executing processes.

**Two modes of learning:**

| Dimension | Learning Before Doing | Learning By Doing |
|---|---|---|
| **Power** | Unleashes the power of science and technology to improve | Unleashes creativity of the whole workforce and its power to improve |
| **Organization type** | Encourages a safe and efficient organization that promotes knowledge sharing and ensures complex processes will work | Encourages a dynamic organization that minimizes planning time |
| **Compatible with** | Step change and even drastic improvement | Continuous improvement process; essential to Toyota's Kaizen system |

### 13. The Learning Curve

**Core concept:** People engaged in repetitive tasks learn by doing. When data on this phenomenon is gathered, a learning curve is found.

**Key properties of the learning curve:**

1. The learning curve shows how **learning by doing translates into improvement**.
2. Empirically derived learning curves indicate that **learning by doing is driven by cumulative volume** -- we learn each time we process a unit, and the learning accumulates.
3. In its strictest interpretation, it depicts the relationship between **direct labor hours per unit** and the **cumulative number of units produced**.
4. A striking empirical observation: the data defines a **linear relationship on a log-log scale** -- i.e., Log(marginal direct labor hour) vs. Log(cumulative volume) appears as a straight line.
5. This means: the amount of direct labor hours needed for one unit of production **reduces by a similar percentage every time cumulative volume doubles**. This percentage is called the **learning rate**.

**Mathematical formulation:**

- **Log-linear form:** log C(Q) = log C1 - beta * log Q
  - C1 = marginal cost of the first unit
  - beta = slope measured on a log-log scale
- **Power form (equivalent):** C(Q) = C1 * Q^(-beta)
- **Learning rate calculation:**
  - C(Q) - C(2Q) = (1 - 2^(-beta)) * C(Q)
  - Learning rate = 1 - 2^(-beta)

---

### 14. Moore's Law and the Learning Curve

**Moore's Law** (Gordon Moore, 1964): The observation that the number of transistors in a dense integrated circuit (IC) doubles about every two years. It is NOT a law of physics but an **empirical relationship linked to gains from experience in production**.

**Intel's Robert Noyce (1977):** Between 1960 and 1977, annual usage of transistors increased by 2000 times (doubled 11 times in 17 years). This promoted continual cost reductions of 28% for each doubling of volume.

**Data table (semiconductor cost dynamics):**

| Year | Bits/die | Line Width (micron) | Cost (millicents/bit) | Cumulative bits sold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1974 | 4,100 | 6 | 8.02 | 2.00 x 10^12 |
| 1977 | 16,000 | 3.5 | 2.89 | 3.00 x 10^13 |
| 1980 | 66,000 | 2.3 | 1.37 | 3.00 x 10^14 |
| 1983 | 260,000 | 1.3 | 0.395 | 2.00 x 10^15 |
| 1986 | 1,100,000 | 1 | 0.196 | 1.00 x 10^16 |
| 1989 | 4,200,000 | 0.8 | 0.105 | 5.00 x 10^16 |
| 1992 | 17,000,000 | 0.5 | 0.038 | 2.70 x 10^17 |
| 1995 | 67,000,000 | 0.35 | 0.019 | 1.25 x 10^18 |
| 1998 | 270,000,000 | 0.25 | 0.01 | 6.25 x 10^18 |

**Calculating the learning rate from the data:**
- Slope beta on log-log scale = 3/7 (derived from the graph)
- Learning rate = 1 - 2^(-3/7) = approximately 25.7%
- This means that each doubling of cumulative volume decreases cost per bit by about 25.7%.

**Graph description:** A log-log plot of Cost per bit (millicents) on the y-axis vs. Cumulative bits "produced" Q on the x-axis. The data points form a nearly straight line sloping downward, confirming the learning curve relationship.

### 15. The Learning Curve -- Uses and Caveats

**Learning is "predictable":** We can extrapolate into the future with reasonable accuracy based on past learning curves.

**The learning curve is useful for:**
- **Capacity planning & budgeting of resources** -- predicting future labor/cost requirements.
- **Checking whether implementation of new processes "is on track"** -- comparing actual performance to predicted learning curve.

**Other learning models (with empirical support):**
- Some models indicate learning is driven by **time** rather than cumulative volume.
- These models stipulate annual productivity growths, annual reductions in defect levels, etc.

**Critical caveat:** Learning and improvement does **NOT "just happen."** It requires deliberate effort, investment, and organizational commitment.

---

### 16. Putting It All Together

**Main topic:** Prepare for the future = build competencies/capabilities for innovation and improvement.

**Two modes of learning map to different strategic outcomes:**

| Learning Mode | Strategic Application | Tool/Approach | Example Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Learning By Doing** | Process improvement | Learning curve, Kaizen, Standardized work | Cost reduction |
| **Learning Before Doing** | Product or process innovation | Design strategies, Plan/Iterate/Select strategies | Functionality improvement |

**The interplay between these two learning modes predicts improvement and innovation dynamics.** Organizations must master both modes and understand when to apply each.

### Moderna's mRNA Platform: How It Works

**Traditional vaccines:** Inject weakened or inactivated virus into the body to trigger an immune response.

**Moderna's mRNA approach:**
- Uses **messenger RNA (mRNA)** -- a molecule carrying a genetic sequence -- wrapped in **lipid nanoparticles (LNPs)** (fats that protect and deliver the mRNA into cells).
- Once injected, the body's own ribosomes read the mRNA code and produce the targeted protein (e.g., the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2).
- The body then mounts an immune response against that protein.
- mRNA does NOT enter the cell nucleus and does NOT interact with DNA -- it is not gene editing.
- **mRNA = "software"** (the instructions); **LNPs = "hardware"** (the delivery mechanism).

**Platform analogy:** "mRNA is a platform like the iPhone. The individual drugs -- preventive vaccines or therapeutic treatments -- are like apps. If we get the platform to work, many apps can be developed on our platform." -- Melissa Moore, Chief Scientific Officer

**Key advantage of the platform model:**
- Similar technologies can be used to develop **multiple medicines in parallel**.
- Learnings from one drug **spill over directly** into others.
- Contrast with traditional pharma: research and clinical trials typically done in sequence, with minimal cross-learning between drug candidates.

### Moderna's Founding and Flagship Pioneering

**Flagship Pioneering** is not a typical VC firm. It operates as an "institutional innovation enterprise" -- it conceives, founds, funds, and owns its portfolio companies.

**Flagship's four-stage process for pioneering innovations:**

1. **Explorations:** Generate "What if?" hypotheses believed to yield breakthrough innovations. (e.g., "What if mRNA could be a drug?")
2. **ProtoCos (Prototype Companies):** If explorations prove promising, Flagship forms teams to test feasibility.
3. **NewCos (New Companies):** If the science validates, Flagship commits significant capital; recruits broader team.
4. **GrowthCos (Growth Companies):** Spun out, begins forging investor relationships and outside partnerships. Some eventually IPO.

**Scientific co-founders:** Robert Langer (MIT) and Derrick Rossi (Harvard/Boston Children's Hospital) provided early scientific direction.

**Flagship's philosophy:** Darwinian process -- creation, iteration, and selection of groundbreaking innovations for which no precedent exists. "Asking 'What if?' propels you far into the future."

### The Decision to Go Digital

Bancel recognized the need for digitization early, driven by a critical incident: a scientist manually entering a nucleotide sequence into an Excel spreadsheet across two large screens. A single wrong cell would have ruined the entire drug design.

**Digital strategy principles:**

1. **Digital from day one:** Much easier to digitize from the start than to retrofit a legacy system.
2. **Business process engineering first, then digitize:** "Digitization only makes sense once the processes are done. If you have crappy analog processes, you'll get crappy digital processes." -- Bancel
3. **Holistic redesign:** Cannot blindly translate manual processes into digital processes; must be redesigned to fit a digital environment, done holistically rather than in silos.
4. **Marcello Damiani** hired as Chief Digital and Operational Excellence Officer (2015), with a dual mandate for process engineering across all departments.

### Building the AI Factory

Moderna built its digital and AI capabilities through a layered, multi-step approach:

**Layer 1: Cloud**
- Operating in the cloud (AWS from 2013) was foundational.
- More secure than local hosting; cheaper than in-house infrastructure; more agile and resilient with better disaster recovery.

**Layer 2: Integration**
- Data harmonized across systems; entered once; flows freely to any team that needs it.
- Lab instruments connected through Internet of Things (IoT) for data integration.
- Eliminated siloed data that destroys efficiency and productivity.

**Layer 3: Automation and Robotics**
- Needed process maturity first before automating.
- "Islands of automation" connected into a larger automated whole once each island was stable.
- Systems operated as manual-automated hybrids until full automation achieved.
- Warning: "Automating too much and too early is dangerous."

**Layer 4: Analytics and AI**
- AI is "the holy grail" -- requires the preceding layers (cloud, integration, automation) to generate structured data.
- Creates a **virtuous cycle**: experiments generate data --> data builds better algorithms --> algorithms improve next generation of medication --> repeat.

**AI deployment stats (Spring 2020):**
- ~20 algorithms in production.
- ~20 algorithms in development and testing.
- MVP model development takes on average 6 months (some as little as 1 month).

**Example AI use case -- Sanger Sequencing:**
- Critical quality check for DNA templates (a single nucleotide change = nonfunctioning protein).
- Off-the-shelf solution couldn't perform in-depth analyses; trained operators had to manually review, introducing human error risk.
- Moderna built a convolutional neural network to predict operator response with superhuman accuracy, saving countless hours.

**Software sourcing strategy:**
- ~85% of undifferentiated tools (finance, HR) were off-the-shelf SaaS (e.g., Workday for HR).
- ~85% of company-specific innovation tools (research, technical development) were custom-built.

**Key quote from Dave Johnson (Head of Informatics, Data Science, and AI):** "For us -- and any company, really -- to be able to be an AI company, it takes sponsorship at the highest company level."

### Making Manufacturing Digital

**Juan Andres** (Chief Technical Operations and Quality Officer; formerly 18 years at Eli Lilly, 12 years at Novartis) brought deep manufacturing expertise.

**Key insight on manufacturing's role:**
- In traditional big pharma: manufacturing is a commodity; its role is to translate uncertainty of R&D and drug approval into certainty of supply (i.e., neutralize variability).
- At Moderna: manufacturing is "almost a source of revenue" because of the platform nature. mRNA as a new class of medicines allows manufacturing to assume a fundamentally different, more strategic role.

**Why digitize manufacturing?** "Turning things digital allows fast growth and learning, ensures quality, and makes replication and standardization possible. It's impossible to learn reliably if you need to work with manual data."

**Decision to build in-house (vertical integration of manufacturing):**
- Moderna's platform was so novel that outsourcing was not warranted -- outside manufacturers lacked the relevant expertise.
- A globally dispersed supply chain works for 1-2 product companies but not for a multi-product pipeline company.
- Despite having zero revenue, they invested ~$125M in the Norwood facility -- about 10% of 2016 year-end cash balance. This required enormous conviction in the platform's success.

**Norwood Plant -- Three Manufacturing Engines:**
1. Pre-clinical R&D
2. Personalized cancer vaccine
3. Clinical area

All three are operationally independent but **digitally connected for learning**: "It's easy to transfer any learning from one to the other."

**What sets Norwood apart:** Not the individual digital technologies (big pharma has those too), but the **integration at all levels**. "The way things come together is what matters about technology, not the technology itself. You make medicine by cross-functional integration."

**Integrated Electronic Batch Records:** Saved time and money across equipment use, testing, and release. Enabled real-time integration with SAP for raw material tracking; facilitated all testing steps including labeling, audit trails, and security controls; captured all batch record exceptions digitally for real-time review and QA. Converted stacks of paper into a fully digital, continually updated system.

### Solving for COVID-19: Speed and Execution

**Why Moderna could move so fast (genome sequence to human trial in ~65 days):**

### Partnering for Scale: The Lonza Decision

Once the vaccine was ready, Moderna needed manufacturing help to serve billions of people.

**Why partner:**
- Moderna's own production capacities were too limited for global demand.
- "If we are successful in developing that vaccine, demand will exceed supply. Enormously!"
- Bancel challenged his team: "Think in 10x dimensions, not incrementally."

**Why Lonza:**
- Scored high on all evaluation criteria.
- Had a U.S. factory only one hour from Norwood (easy cooperation).
- Had an additional facility in Visp, Switzerland (could serve European market).
- Andres had prior personal relationship with Lonza based on trust and shared values.
- Lonza could produce at scale AND was a highly digital company -- two things Moderna needed.
- Technology transfer targeted for completion by July 2020, with production starting in less than 3 months (a process that normally takes 2-3 years).

### The Strategic Dilemma: Separate COVID Organization?

**Arguments for spinning off a separate COVID-19 unit:**
- Could enable exclusive focus on the pandemic response.
- Could allow even faster, more agile, and nimble operations.
- Could be repurposed for future pandemic responses.
- Would prevent COVID from overshadowing Moderna's other 22+ pipeline candidates.

**Arguments against spinning off:**
- Moderna's competitive advantage comes from **shared understanding and shared data** across ALL departments and teams.
- Creating silos was something Moderna was determined to avoid from its founding.
- Risk of losing the cross-learning benefits of the platform model.
- The digital structure was designed specifically to avoid the detrimental effects of silos seen in big pharma.

## PART 3: CONNECTING CASE TO CONCEPTS

---

### How Moderna Illustrates Class 7 Concepts

#### 1. Product Technology and Process Technology

Moderna exemplifies the tight linkage between product technology (mRNA science, LNP delivery) and process technology (digital manufacturing, automated production). The case shows how these cannot be developed in isolation -- the product IS the process in many ways for an mRNA company.

#### 2. Linking Product and Process Development

Moderna practices **concurrent engineering** at its core: drug design (product) and manufacturing process development happen in parallel, not sequentially. The drug design studio represents the fusion of product and process thinking. Their digital platform allows real-time feedback between design and manufacturing.

#### 3. Learning Before Doing vs. Learning By Doing

Moderna employs BOTH modes extensively:
- **Learning before doing:** Years of fundamental mRNA research, LNP research, algorithm development for drug design, and AI model building BEFORE COVID hit. This is why they could respond in days.
- **Learning by doing:** The Norwood plant generates data from every manufacturing run (25,000+ iterations), creating a virtuous learning cycle. Each batch teaches something that improves the next.

#### 4. The Learning Curve in Action

- Moderna's COVID vaccine was their **10th vaccine** -- cumulative experience from 9 prior vaccine programs dramatically reduced the time and effort needed.
- Manufacturing cycle times shortened through learning by doing at the Norwood facility.
- The AI algorithms improved with each iteration -- more data led to better models (analogous to a learning curve).

#### 5. Innovation vs. Improvement

- **Innovation:** mRNA as a drug platform is a radical innovation -- creating something fundamentally new that did not exist before.
- **Improvement:** Continuous improvement through digitization, automation, AI integration, and process refinement at Norwood. Each manufacturing run improves the next.

#### 6. Continuous Improvement Process

Moderna's digital infrastructure embodies CI principles:
- **Scientific method:** Every experiment generates hypotheses tested through data.
- **Variability reduction:** Digital systems eliminate manual errors (e.g., replacing Excel-based nucleotide entry with automated drug design studio).
- **Built-in controls:** AI algorithms for quality checking (Sanger sequencing neural network).
- **Employee empowerment:** Cross-functional integration and shared data access.

#### 7. Reengineering

Moderna itself is an example of reengineering the drug development process:
- They followed the stage-gate approach: Flagship's Explorations --> ProtoCos --> NewCos --> GrowthCos.
- They used partners (Lonza) to manage risk at scale.
- They used simulation (drug design on computer) to test before manufacturing.

#### 8. Copy Best Practice vs. Build New

The Lonza partnership represents **copying best practice** -- replicating Moderna's proven digital manufacturing process at Lonza's facilities. The guidelines apply: work from a single active template (Norwood), copy as closely as possible, don't adapt until reaching acceptable results.

#### 9. Centralized vs. Decentralized Process Development

Moderna chose **centralized** process development at Norwood, then transferred to Lonza. This enabled standardization (important for regulatory compliance and quality), shared learning, and efficiency. The tradeoff: reliance on a single site creates supply chain risk, which the Lonza partnership partially addresses.

#### 10. Platform vs. Product Approach and the S-Curve

Moderna's mRNA platform represents being at the **beginning of a new S-curve** -- a new technological era where performance (number of drugs, speed of development) increases rapidly. This connects to the product life cycle concept: as the mRNA platform matures and product designs stabilize, Moderna can invest more in automation to reduce costs.

#### 11. Digital as Enabler of Learning

The entire Moderna case is a testament to how digital infrastructure accelerates both modes of learning. Digital systems make learning visible, shareable, and cumulative -- turning individual learning into organizational learning. This is the foundation of competitive advantage in operations.

### Key Takeaways and Lessons

1. **Platform thinking transforms operations strategy.** By treating mRNA as a platform (like an operating system), Moderna can develop multiple drugs simultaneously with shared learning -- fundamentally different from traditional pharma's one-product-at-a-time approach.

2. **Invest in capabilities before you need them.** Moderna's investments in digital infrastructure, AI, in-house manufacturing, and pandemic preparedness all happened YEARS before COVID. When the crisis hit, they were ready because they had built the capabilities in advance.

3. **Digitization must start with process redesign.** Simply digitizing bad analog processes creates bad digital processes. Process engineering must come first.

4. **Integration is more important than individual technologies.** Big pharma has great individual technologies too; Moderna's advantage is in how everything connects and how data flows across the organization.

5. **Learning curves apply to knowledge work, not just manufacturing.** Moderna's 10th vaccine was dramatically faster than their first. Cumulative experience in mRNA design, manufacturing, regulatory relationships, and clinical trials all followed learning curve dynamics.

6. **The tension between focus and integration is real.** The case's central dilemma -- whether to spin off a COVID unit -- illustrates the fundamental operations strategy tension between dedicated focus (which enables speed) and integration (which enables cross-learning and avoids silos).

7. **Vertical integration can be a strategic advantage for novel platforms.** When your process is so novel that outsourcers lack expertise, in-house manufacturing enables speed and control. Moderna's Norwood plant was essential to their rapid COVID response.

8. **Scale-up requires partnerships.** Even with in-house capabilities, meeting global demand requires partnerships (Lonza). The key is finding partners who match both your production capabilities AND your digital maturity.

9. **Pre-revenue investment in manufacturing signals conviction.** Spending $125M on a factory when you have zero revenue requires deep conviction in your platform. This kind of bold investment is characteristic of a "learning before doing" strategy.

10. **AI requires a foundation.** Moderna's AI factory was built in layers: cloud --> integration --> automation --> AI. Each layer depends on the one below it. Trying to skip layers leads to failure.

## Course Overview / Central Premise

The course is structured around the idea that operations can be a significant source of competitive advantage. Companies like ZARA, Wal-Mart, Procter & Gamble, Toyota, and Coca-Cola all treat their operational capabilities as integral to their competitive strategy. They invest strategically in:

- Physical plants and facilities
- Process and information technology
- Employee, supplier, and distributor relationships
- Organizational practices and know-how

The module focuses on how to achieve multiple objectives (lowest cost, best quality, prompt delivery, flexibility) through **operations strategy** -- matching demand with supply through strategic choices regarding a firm's processes, technologies, capacity, location, flexibility, timing, learning, and innovation.

---

## Course Objectives (Learning Goals)

After taking this course, students will be able to:

1. **List and explain** the range of strategic choices to be made in operations strategy
2. **List and explain** the various links between operations and other business functions
3. **Analyse and describe** the internal and external factors that influence strategic operational investments
4. **Analyse and describe** the connection between operations strategy and the firm's business strategy and competitive position
5. **List and describe** basic principles that lead to an organization's operational success
6. **Assess** an existing operations strategy by identifying its key elements and evaluating it qualitatively and financially
7. **Improve** an operations strategy by analysing key drivers and decisions for each element and by developing recommendations and implementation plans

## Key Themes of the Course

### 1. The Operations Strategy Framework
The central framework connects **business strategy** (price, quality, responsiveness, variety) to **capabilities** (cost, quality, lead-time, flexibility) through **operations strategy**, which consists of **Resources** (size, timing, type, location) and **Processes** (supply, technology, innovation, demand).

### 2. Three Views of Operations
- **Process View:** How work is done (activity network / transformation process)
- **Resource View:** What assets perform the work (real assets)
- **Competency/Capability View:** What operations can and cannot do

### 3. Alignment
Operations strategy must align with business strategy. Two key alignment tools:
- **Strategic Operational Audit (SOA)**
- **Product-Process Matrix (PPM/OMM)**

### 4. Value Maximization through NPV
The goal of operations strategy is to maximize Net Present Value (NPV). Analytical/financial tools are used throughout to evaluate strategic decisions quantitatively.

### 5. Capacity Decisions
A major portion of the course (Classes 3-6) deals with the four dimensions of capacity strategy:
- **Size** (how much capacity?)
- **Timing** (when to expand/contract?)
- **Type** (what kind of technology/flexibility?)
- **Location** (where to locate resources?)

### 6. Process Decisions
- Supply management (make vs. buy, outsourcing)
- Technology adoption
- Innovation and improvement (learning curves, kaizen, lean)
- Demand and revenue management

### 7. Trade-offs and Competitive Positioning
The course emphasizes that operations must balance competing objectives (cost, quality, speed, flexibility) and that strategic choices involve trade-offs.

### 8. Case-Based Learning
The course relies heavily on case analysis to apply frameworks:
- Swiss Watch Industry (Class 1) -- Introduction to operations strategy
- American Connector Company (Class 2-3) -- Competitive analysis and trade-off curves
- Eli Lilly (Class 5-6) -- Flexibility and capacity type decisions
- Moderna (Class 7) -- Technology and innovation

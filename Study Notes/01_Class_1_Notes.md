## 1. What is Strategy?

### Definition
**Strategy** is a plan (often long-term) to reach a particular goal. For business organizations, the goal of strategy is to **maximize the Net Present Value (NPV)**.

### Three Levels of Strategy (Hierarchical)

Large organizations define strategy in a hierarchical manner across three levels:

| Level | Description | Key Decisions |
|---|---|---|
| **Corporate Strategy** | Highest level; defines the industries in which the organization will be active | Configures business units for each industry; acquires and allocates corporate resources to each business unit (a self-contained part of an organization) |
| **Business Strategy** | Formulated by each business unit to seek a favorable competitive position in the industry | Competes on four aspects: **Price, Quality, Responsiveness, and Variety** -- these describe the value proposition to customers |
| **Functional Strategies** | Defined to execute the business strategy | Includes: financial strategy (how capital is raised/invested), marketing strategy (market segmentation, product positioning/promotion), and **operations strategy** (plan for developing resources and configuring processes for creation and delivery of products/services) |

### Key Insight
- The **industry** is the fundamental arena in which competition occurs.
- Business strategy defines the competitive position within that industry.
- Operations strategy is one of several functional strategies that execute the business strategy.

## 2. What is Operations?

### Definition
The **operations function** is responsible for the **creation and delivery of products and services**.

### Three Complementary Views of Operations

It is helpful to adopt three different views to look at operations:

---

### View 1: The Process View

The process view looks at the **transformation process** (i.e., the activity network) used to create and deliver products and services.

- **Focus:** How work or activity is done
- **Operations Strategy (OS):** Makes long-term decisions related to the transformation process (e.g., make or buy, technology adoption and update, make-to-order vs. make-to-stock, how to match supply with demand)
- **Operations Management (OM):** Manages and improves the day-to-day activities in the transformation process; focuses on tactics (covered in DAO2703, the prerequisite course)

#### Four Key Process Decisions:

1. **Supply -- Which activities should we perform internally vs. outsource?**
   - This is inputs management, the "make or buy" decision
   - Supply chain examples: vertical integration, outsourcing (multi-sourcing or single-sourcing)
   - Note: Technology affects sourcing decisions and supply chain structure

2. **Technology -- Which technology do our processes need?**
   - Examples of process technologies: CAD, CAE, CAM, CAPP, NC, CNC, DNC, CIM, AGVs, Robots, Drones, FMS, GPS, AI
   - Need to link process technology decisions with product technology and product design
   - Case studies that relate: Moderna vs. Eli Lilly

3. **Innovation -- When and how to improve and innovate?**
   - Types of improvement:
     - **Continuous improvement:** Kaizen, Lean, Six Sigma (6-sigma quality)
     - **Radical change:** Copy best practice, reengineering
   - **Organizational learning** is directly related to both improvement and innovation:
     - Two activities: **conceptual learning** (why) and **operational learning** (how)
     - Two modes: **learning before doing** (prototyping) and **learning by doing** (real production)

4. **Demand -- How to match demand with available supply?**
   - This is outputs management; the goal is to maximize revenue
   - Especially important when supply processes cannot quickly adapt to demand changes
   - Tools: **Price control** and **capacity control**
   - **Revenue Management (RM):** The collection of demand management practices that aim to maximize the revenue of especially limited and perishable available supply
   - Marginal analysis is a useful tool in RM
   - Overbooking is an example of RM

---

### View 2: The Resource View

The resource view looks at the **assets used in the transformation process**. To perform and coordinate activities, organizations need a variety of real assets/resources. This view asks: who or what does the activities/work?

#### Four Key Resource Decisions:

1. **Size -- How many resources should we invest?**
   - Examples: Number of plants, capacity size of each plant
   - **Key trade-off:** Cost of excess capacity vs. capacity shortfall (opportunity cost)
   - **Tool:** Marginal analysis can determine the optimal capacity size

2. **Timing -- When should we increase or reduce resources?**
   - Examples: Eli Lilly abandoned a $325 million insulin plant in 2007; Solar-Panel Maker Solyndra closed a factory and delayed expansion in 2010
   - **Key trade-off:** Cost of capacity adjustment vs. excess/shortfall of capacity
   - **Tools:** Strategic timing and tactical timing

3. **Type -- What kinds/type of resources are best?**
   - Examples: Process type (job shop vs. repetitive line), facility and equipment types
   - **Key decision:** Degree of automation and flexibility

4. **Location -- Where should the resources be located?**
   - Examples: 7-Eleven stores, Zara manufacturing plants
   - Note: Globalization is certain to increase the importance of this decision

---

### View 3: The Competency/Capabilities View

The competency view looks at **what operations can and cannot do**. It asks the critical question: which capabilities should an operation have? This view should connect directly to business strategy.

#### Four Key Capability Dimensions:

1. **Cost -- What is the total cost of operating?**
   - Includes fixed and variable costs
   - Especially important in low-margin industries

2. **Quality -- What is the ability to deliver quality outputs?**
   - **Design-related quality:** Performance and features
   - **Performance-related quality:** Reliability and durability
   - Quality is a key differentiator in luxury and high-precision businesses

3. **Lead-Time (Responsiveness) -- What is the total responsiveness or lead-time?**
   - Key success factor in service industries and in rapidly changing environments

4. **Flexibility -- What is operations' flexibility to change?**
   - Examples: Scope (range of products), agility (speed of changeover), robustness (ability to handle disruptions)
   - Key success factor when competing on customized products/services

## 3. The Operations Strategy Framework

### The Framework Diagram

```
Business Strategy: Price, Quality, Responsiveness, Variety
            |
            v
Capabilities: Cost, Quality, Lead-Time, Flexibility
            |
            v
      Operations Strategy
       /              \
  Resources          Processes
  |                  |
  Size               Supply
  Timing             Technology
  Type               Innovation
  Location           Demand
```

(Source: Operations Strategy by Prof. Jan A. Van Mieghem)

### Formal Definition of Operations Strategy

> **Operations Strategy** is a long-term plan for developing resources and configuring processes such that the resulting capabilities maximize Net Present Value (NPV).

### Two Main Questions Operations Strategy Answers:

1. **What should operations be good at?** -- Which capabilities should an organization nurture?
2. **Which operational system of resources and processes best provides these capabilities?**

---

## 4. Alignment

### The Alignment Concept

The first question ("What should operations be good at?") is answered by **connecting or aligning** capabilities with the business strategy.

### Alignment Flow (Two Perspectives):

**Market Perspective (Top-Down):**
1. **Business Strategy:** What do we compete on? How do we provide value to our customers?
2. **Competencies:** Prioritize the customer value proposition around price, responsiveness, quality, variety, etc.
3. Then ask: What must operations do particularly well? Prioritize process competencies around cost, lead time, quality, flexibility, etc.

**Resource Perspective (Bottom-Up):**
1. How do we configure and develop our operations?
2. Configure the activity network (processes)
3. Develop the bundle of real assets (resources)

### The Alignment Principle

> **Operations Strategy should develop resources and configure processes such that the resulting capabilities are connected or aligned with the competitive position (defined by business strategy) that a firm seeks over time.**

## 5. Alignment Tools

### Tool 1: Strategic Operational Audit (SOA)

The SOA is a diagnostic tool that identifies gaps between what a firm promises and what it delivers. It works by examining three potential gaps:

```
Value Proposition  <-- Strategy Gap? -->  Deliverable Value
     (Market view)                          (Market view)

Competencies       <-- Competency Gap? --> Needed Competencies

Resources &        <-- Resource &       --> Needed Resources &
Processes              Process Gap?         Processes
```

**How to use the SOA:**

1. **Starting points:** Identify the firm's **Value Proposition** (what it promises to customers) and then determine what **Deliverable Value** it actually delivers
2. **Strategy Gap:** Is there a gap between what the firm promises and what it delivers?
3. **Competency Gap:** Compare current competencies with the needed competencies to deliver the value proposition
4. **Resource & Process Gap:** Compare current resources and processes with what is needed to create alignment

The SOA helps identify where misalignment exists and guides corrective action.

From personal notes: Firms should continually assess current resources and processes and together determine what capabilities they can actually generate. Then take those capabilities and determine what value proposition they can actually deliver.

---

### Tool 2: Product-Process Matrix (PPM / OMM)

The Product-Process Matrix matches between **process type/technology** and **delivered product attributes/variety**.

```
                     Product Variety
                Low (Standardized)  ------>  High (Customized)
Process        |
Flexibility    |
               |
High           |  Job Shop          C                A
               |
               |  Batch
               |                    B                D
Low            |  Repetitive Line
```

**Zone of Alignment:** Runs diagonally from bottom-left (standardized products, repetitive line, low flexibility) to top-right (customized solutions, job shop, high flexibility).

**Interpretation of positions:**
- **Positions along the diagonal (B):** Aligned -- process flexibility matches product variety
- **Position A (top-right):** High flexibility AND high variety -- potentially over-investing in flexibility
- **Position C (top-left):** High flexibility but standardized products -- mismatch, overspending on flexible capacity for products that don't need it
- **Position D (bottom-right):** Low flexibility but customized products -- mismatch, cannot deliver the variety customers need

**Types of Resource Production (from personal notes):**
- **Project:** One-off, unique projects
- **Job Shop:** Different path for each job; has flexibility and each has different processes, but all still in the same facility (e.g., a grocery store)
- **Batch:** Like repetitive, but made in batches of a certain quantity
- **Repetitive/Line:** Continuous flow of identical products
- **Continuous:** E.g., petroleum processing

From personal notes: The key decision for supply processes is whether to outsource or make in-house.

## 6. Net Present Value (NPV)

### Why NPV?

The goal of operations strategy is to maximize NPV. This is the fundamental financial metric for evaluating strategic operational investments.

### NPV Formula

```
NPV = C_0 + PV
```

Where:
- **C_0** = Initial investment costs (typically a negative number, representing cash outflow at time 0)
- **PV** = Present Value of future cash flows

### Present Value Formula

```
PV = Sum from t=1 to T of [ C_t / (1 + r)^t ]
```

Where:
- **r** = Rate of return (called the **discount rate**)
- **C_t** = Return cash flows in future time period t
- **T** = Total number of time periods

### Key Concepts:
- A dollar today is worth more than a dollar in the future (time value of money)
- The discount rate reflects the opportunity cost of capital and the risk of the investment
- Positive NPV means the investment creates value; negative NPV means it destroys value
- When comparing operational strategies, choose the one with the highest NPV

### From Personal Notes:
Once we have the fundamentals down (framework, alignment), we need to choose the operational system that achieves the highest NPV for the firm.

## 7. Mini-Case: The Swiss Watch Industry

### Part I: Background and the Crisis

#### The Traditional Swiss Watch Industry

- Swiss watches were legendary and once dominated the world market.
- In the mid-1970s, 43% of units sold worldwide were Swiss made.
- Then things changed dramatically.

#### The Worldwide Watch Industry

- During the late 1970s, manufacturers from Japan and Hong Kong penetrated the watch market by using low-cost labor, quartz technology (developed but not used in Switzerland), and mass production.
- Their inexpensive watches quickly gained an important market share.
- In compensation for lost revenues from diminished market share, Swiss watchmakers raised prices.
- By 1981, the world market for watches was about 500 million units per year and consisted of three segments:
  - **Low-end segment:** Prices up to about $75; represented 450 million units out of 500 million
  - **Middle segment:** Prices up to $450; represented 42 million units
  - **High-end/luxury segment:** 8 million watches for the top segment
- **Market share facts:**
  - Margins retreat left the Swiss with zero share of the low-end segment
  - Only 3% of the middle segment
  - But 97% share of the luxury watches (which accounted for 1.6% of world unit watch sales)

#### By 1983:
- Swiss share plunged to less than 15%, and 1,000 of the 1,600 Swiss watch firms closed.
- These massive closures were accompanied by a contraction of the labor force by two-thirds.

#### The Banks' Intervention:
- At the brink of bankruptcy, the creditor banks of the two flagship companies of the Swiss watch industry (Swiss-French SSIH and Swiss-German ASUAG) asked the consulting firm Hayek Engineering for advice: "Should we shut down or can you design a new operations strategy?"

#### Watch Technology Background

**Mechanical watches** consist of two groups of parts:
- The **visible exterior parts** (case, dial, hands)
- The **interior "movement"** which contains about 200 individual parts and is driven merely by a spring

After a long period, two main mechanical watch technologies emerged:
- The **lever** was used for expensive watches -- then jewels tipped the teeth in the movement
- The **pin pallet** was used for cheaper watches

**Traditional Swiss production:**
- The process to produce and assemble mechanical watches was labor-intensive
- Master artisans would work in a library-style environment on well-lit work desks with microscopes where they would compile a piece of "living art" worth more than most of its parts
- In the mid-20th century, most watches sold in the US were bought for under $50
- Full production cost consisted of approximately 60% labor, 20% materials, and 20% depreciation

**The First Electric Watch (1957):**
- Hamilton Watch Co. of Lancaster, Pennsylvania produced the world's first electric watch
- It still used a traditional balance-wheel mechanism but used a battery instead of a spring as power source

**The Electronic Revolution (1960s):**
- In 1960, the next advance in electric watches came from Bulova (founded 1875 in NYC by Joseph Bulova, a 23-year-old immigrant from Bohemia)
- The Bulova corporation replaced the balance-wheel mechanism with the electronic vibrations of a tuning fork
- It raised the beat of the watch from 2.5 beats per minute (mechanical) to nearly 2.5 million per second for an electronic watch
- The tuning fork was kept vibrating by a battery-powered electronic circuit
- The Bulova electronic watch was one of the first consumer products to use a transistor

#### The Japanese Watch Industry

- The electronic watch technology was embraced and enhanced by the Japanese watch industry.
- Since its inception in 1880, the Japanese watch industry was concentrated in the hands of three competitors: K. Hattori (which marketed the Seiko brand), Citizen, and Orient.
- The three companies accounted for 50%, 30%, and 20% of production in 1990, respectively.
- Japanese manufacturers used skilled labor commanding wages at a level of approximately 1/10 of those in Switzerland.

**Seiko's Strategy:**
- During the 1950s, Seiko pursued the goal of closing the technological gap between Swiss products and its own products
- Also developed a policy of standardizing the movements of the different watches and invested heavily in automatic equipment
- This permitted Seiko to reduce labor to 35% of the full production cost of a watch in the late 1960s
- Vertical integration of component manufacturing also permitted more rapid deployment of new technology and watch design
- One industry observer estimated that the Japanese enjoyed a 15% to 45% variable cost advantage over the Swiss in the mid-1960s
- During the late 1960s they initiated component fabrication and assembly operations abroad, in Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia
- Workers in all three locations earned about one-third as much as did Japanese workers
- In the early 1970s, Hattori-Seiko entered the American and European markets

**Swiss Research and Quartz Technology:**
- Meanwhile, the Swiss research lab CEH (Centre Electronique Horloger) developed a more accurate oscillator and produced the first wristwatch quartz movement in 1967
- In contrast to the Swiss manufacturers who had refined their mechanical technology to catch up with Bulova, Seiko made a commitment to develop and produce both analog and digital Quartz watches
- The popularity of quartz watches began to grow around 1976
- By 1977, Seiko became the largest watch company in the world

1. How would you contrast the operations strategy of the traditional Swiss watch company with that of Seiko?
2. What information would you gather and what advice would you give? Would you close shop or recommend a new strategy for the Swiss watch industry? For the latter, use the framework describing your recommended value proposition and required changes in competencies and the operational system.

### Part II: The Turnaround and Swatch (What Actually Happened)

#### Hayek Engineering's Discovery
- In a worldwide market and consumer psychology study, Hayek Engineering discovered that the same watch model would sell substantially better if it carried the "Made in Switzerland" label.
- For a Swiss watch, **75%-95% of European consumers** were willing to pay a **7%-10% premium** over one made in Japan, and **20% premium** over one made in Hong Kong.
- In the U.S., between **51%-75%** of consumers (depending on region) were willing to pay such a premium.
- In Japan, the majority of consumers preferred Japanese watches.

#### The Conventional Wisdom (Which Hayek Rejected)
- The general consensus in Switzerland was that low-cost production was impossible in Western Europe, and even if possible, not desirable because it would hurt sales of high-end watches.

#### Nicolas Hayek's Philosophy
- Hayek believed: "We must build where we live. When a country loses the know-how and expertise to manufacture things, it loses its capacity to create wealth -- its financial independence. When it loses its financial independence, it starts to lose political sovereignty."
- He concluded that **the low end of the market must be controlled**; otherwise there was no hope for survival in the long run.

#### The Strategy
The only way to achieve this goal was by:
1. **Completely redesigning the clockwork**
2. **Drastically reducing and standardizing the number of parts** (quartz resonators, stepping motors, etc.)
3. **Revolutionizing manufacturing**

#### The Merger and Formation of SMH
- Hayek recommended that Switzerland's banks merge the country's two giant (and insolvent) watch manufacturers.
- The plan: **cut total labor cost to less than 10% of total manufacturing cost** (down from 60% in the traditional approach).
- Banks were reluctant; they were ready to write off losses.
- Banks asked Hayek to use his own money. He assembled a group of investors, retained the single largest stake by purchasing **15% of the equity (SFr 80 million)**.
- He became CEO of the newly founded **Societe de Microelectronique et d'Horlogerie (SMH)** -- Swiss Corporation for Microelectronics and Watchmaking.

#### Swatch: The Product
- SMH's answer was **Swatch** -- a slim plastic watch with **only 51 components** that combined top quality with a highly affordable price.
- First went on sale in **1983 for about $40**.
- Demand increased dramatically.
- Swatch offered people a style and a message of "joy de vivre" -- provocative, high-quality Swiss culture at low cost.
- The combination of affordable fashion and emotion turned Swatch into **the most successful wristwatch of all time** with more than **200 million units sold by 2000**.

#### ETA and Vertical Integration
- **ETA** was the clockworks and movement manufacturer for almost all SMH watches (including Swatch and Omega).
- SMH was **almost fully vertically integrated**.
- For electronic movements: mastered semiconductor technology, quartz, batteries, miniaturization, specialized injection molding, and automated assembly.
- **From midnight to 8AM, the assembly line ran practically without human intervention** (high automation).
- SMH also nurtured intricate micro mechanics and the old art of watchmaking for mechanical movements in the luxury segment (maintained dual capability).

#### Competitive Position in Movements Market
- Hong Kong: Produces only 110 million movements but sells 360 million finished watches (massive importer of movements).
- Japan: Manufactures 325 million movements but sells only 50 million watches (massive exporter of movements).
- Japan was selling movements at very low prices: SFr 1.5 to SFr 3.
- Hong Kong became dependent on Japanese supply of movements.
- **Swatch began selling movements to Hong Kong for SFr 3.2** and gained control in this market too -- undercutting Japanese dominance by offering Swiss-made quality at competitive prices.

## 10. Key Lessons from the Swiss Watch Case

### Lesson 1: Operations Strategy Can Transform an Industry
- Hayek's turnaround of the Swiss watch industry was fundamentally an operations strategy transformation, not just a marketing or product design change.
- By redesigning the clockwork (reducing from ~200 parts to 51 parts), revolutionizing manufacturing, and cutting labor to <10% of cost, Swatch competed on cost while maintaining the "Swiss Made" brand premium.

### Lesson 2: Controlling the Low End is Strategically Critical
- Hayek's key insight was that the low end of the market must be controlled, or the high end would eventually fall too. This is a lesson about market strategy being linked to operations capability.

### Lesson 3: Alignment Between Strategy and Operations is Essential
- The traditional Swiss industry was aligned (high quality/high flexibility for luxury watches) but was disrupted when the market shifted.
- Seiko was aligned (low cost/automation for mass market).
- SMH/Swatch created a new alignment: Swiss quality brand + automated low-cost production = affordable fashion watches.

### Lesson 4: Vertical Integration as Competitive Weapon
- SMH's vertical integration in both electronic and mechanical movements gave it independence from Japanese component suppliers and even allowed it to sell movements to Hong Kong, turning a defensive position into an offensive one.

### Lesson 5: Technology Choices Have Long-Term Strategic Consequences
- The Swiss developed quartz technology (CEH lab, 1967) but failed to commercialize it. The Japanese did.
- This illustrates the gap between innovation capability and innovation strategy.

### Lesson 6: The Framework Can Analyze Any Industry
- By systematically examining the Resource View (size, timing, type, location), Process View (supply, technology, innovation, demand), and Capabilities View (cost, quality, lead-time, flexibility), we can describe, compare, and improve any firm's operations strategy.

## 12. Frameworks and Models Summary

### The Operations Strategy Framework
- **Top level:** Business Strategy (Price, Quality, Responsiveness, Variety)
- **Middle level:** Capabilities (Cost, Quality, Lead-Time, Flexibility)
- **Bottom level:** Operations Strategy = Resources (Size, Timing, Type, Location) + Processes (Supply, Technology, Innovation, Demand)

### Strategic Operational Audit (SOA)
- Identifies three types of gaps: Strategy Gap, Competency Gap, Resource & Process Gap
- Used to diagnose misalignment between what a firm promises and what it can deliver

### Product-Process Matrix (PPM/OMM)
- X-axis: Product Variety (low/standardized to high/customized)
- Y-axis: Process Flexibility (low/repetitive line to high/job shop)
- Alignment zone runs diagonally from bottom-left to top-right

### NPV Calculation
- NPV = C_0 + PV
- PV = Sum of [C_t / (1 + r)^t] for t = 1 to T
- Used to evaluate which operational system best provides needed capabilities

## 13. Important Relationships to Remember

1. **Business Strategy drives Capabilities drives Operations Strategy** -- this is the top-down alignment logic
2. **Operations Strategy = Resources + Processes** -- these are the two building blocks
3. **Resources have 4 dimensions:** Size, Timing, Type, Location
4. **Processes have 4 dimensions:** Supply, Technology, Innovation, Demand
5. **Capabilities have 4 dimensions:** Cost, Quality, Lead-Time, Flexibility
6. **Business Strategy has 4 dimensions:** Price, Quality, Responsiveness, Variety
7. The mapping: Price <-> Cost; Quality <-> Quality; Responsiveness <-> Lead-Time; Variety <-> Flexibility
8. **Operations Strategy differs from Operations Management:** OS = long-term strategic decisions; OM = day-to-day tactical management
9. **NPV is the ultimate measure** of an operations strategy's value
10. **Alignment is the guiding principle** -- all elements must connect coherently

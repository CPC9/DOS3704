## 2. What Drives Capacity Location and Globalization?

There are six major categories of drivers that determine where a firm should locate its capacity. Understanding all six is essential for making informed location decisions.

### 2.1 Competitive Strategy

- **Product/Service positioning:** Where the firm positions itself in the market (cost leader, differentiator, niche) influences where it should locate. A cost leader may seek low-cost regions; a differentiator may locate near innovation hubs.
- **Competitors' positions:** The locations chosen by competitors affect your own location decisions (e.g., clustering effects, competitive proximity).

### 2.2 Product Markets

- **Predicted growth, uncertainty, and geographical distribution of demand:** Location decisions should reflect where demand is expected to grow, how uncertain that growth is, and how demand is distributed geographically.
- **Customer proximity premium:** Some products/services command a premium when delivered from a nearby location (freshness, speed, local trust). This premium justifies the cost of distributed networks.
- **Distinctiveness of local product markets:** Different regions may have different tastes, regulations, or requirements that make local presence important.

### 2.3 Supply Markets

- **Availability and costs of inputs:** This includes capital, labor, energy, materials, goods, and services. Locating near cheap or abundant inputs can be a powerful cost advantage.

### 2.4 Technology

- **Production economies (or diseconomies) of scale, scope, and learning:** If there are large economies of scale, centralization is favored. If there are diseconomies of scale, distributed facilities may be preferred. Economies of scope (producing multiple products) and learning (experience curves) also matter.
- **Transportation costs and lead times:** High transportation costs relative to product value favor distributed production near customers. Low transportation costs favor centralized production.

### 2.5 Macro-Economic and Non-Market Factors

- **Tariffs, duties, quotas, and trade agreements:** Trade barriers can make it necessary to produce locally in certain markets.
- **Tax rates and government incentives:** Tax holidays, subsidies, and grants can make certain locations financially attractive.
- **Exchange rates and volatility:** Currency fluctuations affect the relative cost of production in different countries. Volatile exchange rates increase the value of having a flexible global network.
- **IP (Intellectual Property) protection:** Weak IP protection in a region increases the risk of locating there.
- **Political stability:** Unstable political environments add risk to facility investments.

### 2.6 Existing Resources and Processes

- **Existing facility network:** Decisions are not made in a vacuum; the current network constrains and influences new location choices.
- **Existing skills and capabilities:** Available workforce skills and organizational capabilities at existing sites matter.

## 3. Integrating Capacity Location with Operations Strategy

### 3.1 Core Principle

Location decisions are **intimately related** to an organization's operations strategy and must be **integrated with competitive strategy**.

### 3.2 Strategic Alignment Examples

| Competitive Strategy | Location Implication |
|---|---|
| **Cost reduction** | Locate near the source of resources (cheap labor, raw materials, energy) |
| **Convenience for customers** | Many locations in high-traffic areas |

### 3.3 Location Decisions Impact All Dimensions of Operations Strategy (and Vice Versa)

Location decisions have bidirectional relationships with:

- **Competencies:** Costs, responsiveness (lead time), quality, flexibility -- the location chosen affects the firm's ability to compete on each of these dimensions.
- **Capacity Size:** Investment and capital requirements, taxes and incentives, storage and inventory costs all vary by location.
- **Capacity Timing:** Lead time to start up a new facility, ramp-up and ramp-down time differ by location.
- **Capacity Type:** The degree of flexibility and automation achievable depends on local labor markets, infrastructure, and regulations.
- **Supply and Demand:** Labor factors, suppliers and degree of vertical integration, supply chain configuration, market demand factors, and macroeconomic/non-market factors are all location-dependent.
- **Innovation and Technology:** Availability of knowledge, skills, and the state of infrastructure (transport, telecom, utilities) at a location.

## 4. Network Choices

There are three fundamental binary network design choices. Each involves trade-offs.

### 4.1 Centralized vs. Distributed

**Key Question:** Should a network be centralized or distributed?

**Distributed Network:**
- Offers **customer proximity** -- being close to customers provides faster delivery, better service, and a basis for differentiation.
- May be necessary when transportation costs are high relative to product value.

**Centralized Network:**
- Typically observed when **"bigger is better"** -- economies of scale favor fewer, larger facilities.
- Benefits from **risk pooling** -- aggregating volume and demand volatilities across regions reduces the total safety stock and safety capacity needed.
- Can sometimes better guarantee **consistent and uniform quality** because there is one facility to control rather than many.

**Cost Trade-off:**
- Total network costs per unit include two opposing sets of costs:
  - Costs that **decrease** with the size of the served area (e.g., production costs due to economies of scale, inventory costs due to risk pooling)
  - Costs that **increase** with the size of the served area (e.g., transportation costs, lead times, loss of local responsiveness)
- The optimal network design balances these opposing cost trends.

### 4.2 Separated vs. Integrated

**Key Question:** Should the network be separated or integrated?

**Separation** leads to:
- **Market-focused plants** that serve local product and supply markets, resulting in operational efficiencies. This is valuable if customers are willing to pay for local focus.
- **Plant networks that are easy to manage** -- each plant has a clear, simple mandate.

**Integration** (for distributed facilities) means that one location can serve multiple markets, or multiple locations can serve one market. Integration:
- Offers the **scale economies of capacity pooling** -- less safety capacity is needed because demand variability is pooled across markets.
- Comes in **different levels of operational flexibility** (i.e., the degree of integration):
  - Flexible capacity that can produce for multiple markets
  - Contingent allocation of capacity to markets as needed
  - Transshipments between facilities as needed
- Allows **reaction to demand and exchange rate shifts** -- the firm can shift production to the most favorable location as demand patterns or currency values change (managing both demand risk and currency risk).

**Critical insight:** Integration becomes more valuable when demands and exchange rates become **more volatile**. In stable environments, the extra complexity of integration may not be worth it; in volatile environments, the flexibility to reallocate production is extremely valuable.

### 4.3 Standardized vs. Localized

**Key Question:** Should the network be standardized or localized?

**Standardization:**
- Offers **purchasing power** for materials and equipment (bulk buying across facilities).
- Delivers **consistent and uniform quality** across the network.
- **Facilitates integration** and its associated advantages (e.g., risk pooling, capacity sharing), because standardized processes and equipment make it easier to shift production between facilities.
- Can **slow down improvements** because enforcing standards inhibits natural experimentation at individual plants.
- But can also **increase the impact of improvements** -- when one facility discovers a better process, it can be easily copied to all other standardized facilities.

**Localization** (for a distributed network):
- May be **required by regulation** (e.g., local content requirements).
- May be **useful to adjust facilities to available inputs/suppliers** -- examples:
  - Mittal Steel mini-mills: adapted to local scrap metal availability
  - MBA Polymers plastic recycling plants: adapted to local waste streams
- Can be part of a **differentiation strategy** that takes advantage of local customer preferences -- example:
  - Pirelli producing 30-inch tires specifically for the USA market where larger vehicles are common.

## 5. Decision Guidelines for Network Design

1. **Tailor the locations in your network** to fit your strategies and local conditions. One size does not fit all.
2. **Think globally: consider both costs and revenues.**
   - Globalization is certain to increase.
   - Re-optimization of the value chain by including offshore locations can reduce cost by up to 70% (McKinsey estimate).
   - Cutting costs allows companies to decrease prices -- a price drop from $10,000 to $7,000 for a car can double the demand (Farrell, 2004).
3. **Global network flexibility** (the degree of integration) is valuable -- having the ability to shift production across locations in response to changing conditions is a strategic asset.

## 6. Four Types of Location Analysis

### Overview

| # | Analysis Type | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Geographic/Spatial Analysis & TLC | Cost-based comparison of specific sites |
| 2 | Network Analysis | Interdependencies among multiple facilities |
| 3 | Competitive Analysis | Competitor locations and reactions |
| 4 | Facility Role/Focus Analysis | Strategic role and competence of each site |

---

### 6.1 Geographic or Spatial Analysis

**Definition:** An analysis that starts with estimates of market demand per region and of the number of needed potential facilities, and ends by comparing all potential sites' Total Landed Costs (TLC).

**When to use:** When transportation costs constitute a significant fraction of total costs, such as in:
- Cement industry
- Fiberglass insulation industry
- Fresh and frozen food industry

**Example:** A global appliance manufacturer used a density map of its US customer base, along with other information, to make decisions about where to offer premium service operations. Such geographic density maps help identify clusters of demand.

#### Total Landed Cost (TLC)

**Definition:** The total end-to-end cost to transform inputs at the sourcing location to outputs at customer locations. The scope of TLC analysis covers all combinations of products and customers.

**Key characteristics of TLC:**
- TLC has a **cost focus** -- it is fundamentally about minimizing total delivered cost.
- Because TLC focuses on cost, it is important to first **fix the differentiation level** on dimensions of time, quality, and variety/customization, and then estimate what it would take to reach that same level of differentiation in different locations. This ensures you are comparing equivalent service levels across locations.
- The challenge is to **carefully track direct and indirect (often hidden) costs** through the entire value chain. This analysis is described as "painful (because data is not always easy to find) but rewarding."
- TLC analysis is some of the **most quantitative work** in operations strategy.
- It includes: costs of inputs, transportation, labor, inventory, and more.
- A good TLC analysis can save approximately **5% on COGS** (Cost of Goods Sold).

---

### 6.2 Network Analysis

**Definition:** Considers the entirety of a company's facility locations and their interconnections. It can be used in conjunction with spatial analysis.

**Key capability:** Can capture the **interdependency with size and timing** -- how the choice of one facility location affects the optimal choice for future facilities.

**Example (Sequential Decision-Making):**
- You are starting operations in the US.
- If you have a **short-term view:** build one plant at location A (the best standalone location).
- If, however, you **anticipate potential future additions:** you should start at location B, so that in the longer term you can add location C, and TLC(B+C) < TLC(A). This means the combined cost of the two-plant network (B+C) is lower than the single best plant (A), even though B alone might not be the cheapest first choice.
- **Key insight:** The optimal location for the first plant depends on your long-term network expansion plans. Myopic (short-term) decisions can lead to suboptimal networks.

---

### 6.3 Competitive Analysis

**Definition:** Takes the location of competitors or their possible reactions into account when making location decisions.

**Key concepts:**
- Competitors may locate **side by side** in the middle of a main street (e.g., car dealers clustering together).
- This co-location of competitors is consistent with predictions from **game theory**, specifically the **Hotelling model** (Harold Hotelling, 1929). Hotelling's model shows that under certain conditions, competing firms will tend to locate at the center of a market to maximize their share.
- Competitive analysis is especially important when **customers' choice is based on proximity** -- if customers go to the nearest provider, then location relative to competitors is critical.

---

### 6.4 Facility Role or Focus Analysis

**Definition:** The process of specifying the strategic role, activities, responsibilities, and competences of each specific site in the network.

**Key principles:**
- Facility location strategy needs to specify the **role and competences** of a specific site.
- To decide on the role, you must **assign activities and responsibilities** to individual facilities.

**Industry examples of role assignment:**
- The **electronics industry** often separates and assigns R&D, component manufacturing, assembly, and testing to different types of plants: parent plants, component plants, assembly locations, and test locations.
- **Eli Lilly** separates bulk pharmaceutical manufacturing from fill-finish-and-format (fff) plants.

**Key insight:** The strategic role of a facility not only assigns activities but also **clearly specifies responsibilities and competences** as part of the global network. It is not enough to say what a plant does; you must also specify what authority and capabilities it has.

## 7. Ferdows' Strategic Role Matrix for Facilities (1997)

### 7.1 The Framework

Ferdows (1997) suggests that managers start their facility focus analysis by answering two questions:

1. **What is the primary strategic reason for the facility's location?**
   - Three possible reasons (columns): Access to low-cost production | Access to skills and knowledge | Access to market

2. **What is the scope of its current activities and competences?**
   - Two levels (rows): Narrow & low | Broad & high

This creates a **2 x 3 matrix** with six distinct facility roles:

### 7.2 The Six Facility Roles

#### Low Scope of Activities and Competencies (Narrow & Low)

| Role | Primary Strategic Reason | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Offshore Plant** | Access to low-cost production | Produces specific items at low cost and exports them for further work or for sale. Has **minimal authority and investment**. This is the simplest, lowest-autonomy role. |
| **Outpost Plant** | Access to skills and knowledge | Primary role is to **collect information** from advanced suppliers, competitors, research labs, or customers. It acts as a "listening post" or intelligence-gathering facility. Secondary role may be as an offshore plant or server plant. |
| **Server Plant** | Access to market | Serves a **specific regional market** to overcome tariffs, taxes, logistics barriers, or foreign-exchange risk. Has **limited authority** to make minor modifications to fit local conditions. |

#### High Scope of Activities and Competencies (Broad & High)

| Role | Primary Strategic Reason | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Source Plant** | Access to low-cost production | Produces at low cost but with the **same ability as the best plant in the network**. Has **authority over procurement, production, planning, logistics, process and product customization**. This is a high-capability, low-cost plant. |
| **Lead Plant** | Access to skills and knowledge | **Global hub for product and process knowledge and innovation.** Has **full authority over all activities**. Taps into local skills and knowledge to **initiate company-wide innovation**. This is the highest-autonomy, most strategically important role. |
| **Contributor Plant** | Access to market | Serves a **specific regional market** but also **competes with other plants** in the network for new processes or products. Has **authority over product and process development** as well as supplier choice. This is a market-serving plant that also contributes to the network's innovation. |

### 7.3 Key Insights from the Ferdows Matrix

- Plants can **evolve** from lower to higher roles over time (e.g., an offshore plant can develop capabilities and become a source plant).
- The matrix helps managers **articulate** the strategic purpose of each facility and ensure that the facility's mandate, resources, and authority are aligned with that purpose.
- Different roles require different levels of **investment in local capabilities** -- a lead plant needs significant R&D investment; an offshore plant needs minimal investment beyond production equipment.
- The matrix applies to **global networks** -- a multinational firm might have facilities playing different roles in different countries, and the portfolio of roles should be designed to serve the overall competitive strategy.

## 8. Key Terms and Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Total Landed Cost (TLC)** | Total end-to-end cost to transform inputs at the sourcing location to outputs at customer locations, across all products and customers. |
| **Network Analysis** | Analysis of the entirety of a company's facility locations and interconnections, capturing interdependencies with size and timing. |
| **Competitive Analysis** | Location analysis that takes competitors' locations and potential reactions into account. |
| **Hotelling Model** | A game-theoretic model (1929) predicting that competing firms will tend to co-locate at the center of a market. |
| **Facility Role/Focus Analysis** | The process of specifying the strategic role, activities, responsibilities, and competences of each site in a network. |
| **Risk Pooling** | The statistical benefit of aggregating demand across locations, reducing total safety stock/capacity needed. |
| **Capacity Pooling** | Sharing capacity across markets or products to reduce the total capacity required (a form of risk pooling). |
| **Economies of Scale** | Cost advantages from increasing the volume of production at a single facility. |
| **Economies of Scope** | Cost advantages from producing multiple products at a single facility. |
| **Offshore Plant** | Low-cost production facility with minimal authority and investment. |
| **Source Plant** | Low-cost production facility with high capabilities and authority over procurement, production, and logistics. |
| **Outpost Plant** | Intelligence-gathering facility located near advanced suppliers, competitors, or research labs. |
| **Lead Plant** | Global hub for innovation with full authority over all activities. |
| **Server Plant** | Market-serving facility with limited authority, designed to overcome local trade barriers. |
| **Contributor Plant** | Market-serving facility that also competes for new processes and products within the network. |
| **Customer Proximity Premium** | The extra value customers place on being served from a nearby location. |
| **Centralized Network** | A network with few, large facilities serving broad geographic areas. |
| **Distributed Network** | A network with many facilities, each serving a local or regional area. |
| **Standardization** | Making facilities, processes, and equipment uniform across the network. |
| **Localization** | Adapting facilities, processes, or products to local conditions, inputs, or customer preferences. |
| **Integration (Network)** | Designing facilities so that one location can serve multiple markets, or multiple locations can serve one market. |
| **Separation (Network)** | Each facility serves its own local market with no cross-serving. |

## 9. Diagrams and Models Described

### 9.1 Cost Trade-off Curve (Centralized vs. Distributed)

- **X-axis:** Size of the served area (or number of facilities)
- **Y-axis:** Total network cost per unit
- **Two curves:**
  - One set of costs **decreases** with the size of the served area (economies of scale, risk pooling)
  - Another set of costs **increases** with the size of the served area (transportation, lead times)
- The **optimal network** is where the total of both sets of costs is minimized.

### 9.2 Network Analysis Example Diagram

- Shows locations A, B, and C on a map.
- Short-term optimal: Build one plant at A.
- Long-term optimal: Start at B, later add C, because TLC(B+C) < TLC(A).
- Illustrates the importance of forward-looking, dynamic network planning.

### 9.3 Ferdows' 2x3 Strategic Role Matrix

- **Rows:** Scope of activities and competencies (Narrow & Low to Broad & High)
- **Columns:** Primary strategic reason for location (Access to low-cost production | Access to skills and knowledge | Access to market)
- **Six cells:** Offshore, Outpost, Server (bottom row); Source, Lead, Contributor (top row)
- Shows the evolution path: facilities can move from bottom row (low scope) to top row (broad scope) as they develop capabilities.

### 9.4 Operations Strategy Framework

- Shows that Location decisions sit alongside other key operations strategy elements:
  - **Resources (Asset Portfolio):** Size, Time, Type
  - **Processes (Activity Network):** Technology, Innovation, Supply, Demand
  - All guided by **Competitive Strategy** and aimed at building **Competencies**
  - Location is at the center, connecting resources and processes.

## 10. Key Points Emphasized

1. **Location decisions cannot be made in isolation** -- they must be integrated with the firm's competitive strategy and all other elements of operations strategy.
2. **There is no universally "best" network structure** -- the right choice (centralized vs. distributed, standardized vs. localized, separated vs. integrated) depends on the specific competitive strategy, industry economics, and external environment.
3. **Globalization increases the importance of location decisions** -- as markets become more global, the opportunities (and risks) of locating in different countries increase.
4. **Flexibility has value** -- an integrated, flexible global network can respond to changing demand patterns and exchange rates, and this flexibility becomes more valuable as volatility increases.
5. **TLC analysis is quantitative and data-intensive** but can yield significant cost savings (approximately 5% of COGS).
6. **Each facility in a network should have a clearly defined strategic role** (Ferdows matrix) with appropriate authority, investment, and mandate.
7. **Forward-looking network planning** (network analysis) can yield better outcomes than myopic, one-facility-at-a-time decisions.
